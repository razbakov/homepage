# Daily — Why You Can't Fly: Your Brain Is the Limit, Not Physics

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-why-you-cant-fly-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

You can't fly. You read that and didn't flinch. That's the problem. Not gravity — the fact that your brain filed "impossible" before you even thought about it. Most of what you'll never attempt has nothing to do with physics.

`[B-ROLL: Looking up at birds through a glass ceiling, camera slowly tilting]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Your brain is the limit, not physics."]`

## Point 1 — The Dome (0:20–1:10)

Picture yourself standing inside a glass dome. You can see the sky. You can see the birds. But the glass is etched with every "be realistic" you've ever heard. Every credential you thought you needed first. Every adult who told you to stay in your lane. And you've walked the perimeter of that dome so many times you started calling it "the world."

The Wright brothers were called crazy — by people with real PhDs and real math. Semmelweis was institutionalized for saying doctors should wash their hands. The math wasn't wrong. It was incomplete. The ceiling in every one of those stories was never nature. It was a model of nature that everyone mistook for the real thing.

That's the distinction I keep coming back to. The ceiling is not the sky. It's the glass.

`[B-ROLL: Time-lapse of a city skyline seen through a window, reflections visible on the glass]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The ceiling is a model — not nature itself."]`

## Point 2 — Collective Glass (1:10–2:10)

Here's why the dome feels so solid. It's not just in your head. It's in every head around you running the same model. There's an old word for this — egregore. A shared thought-form that pushes back when you try to walk through it.

You know the feeling. The voice that says you need one job. The voice that says you can't run multiple things at once. The voice that says serious adults don't experiment outside their expertise. None of that is physics. It's an agreement between brains. But enough brains agreeing on something — it starts to feel exactly like a wall.

Until someone walks through it. And you realize it was always a curtain.

Here's the trap, though. The smarter you are, the taller your dome. A teenager will try things an expert won't — because the expert has a sharper, more elaborate model of why it can't work. Competence builds the cage.

`[B-ROLL: Crowd walking in sync through a corridor, one person stopping and looking sideways]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Most 'I can't' sentences are curtains, not walls."]`

## Point 3 — Testing the Glass (2:10–3:05)

So what do you actually do with this? I don't pretend the dome isn't there. I do three things.

One — I name the limit before I accept it. "I can't run three projects" becomes "the version of me that thinks in terms of one job at a time can't run three projects." Honest sentence. But it leaves a door open.

Two — I look for one person who's already through that wall. Just one. Their existence cracks the glass. Not because they prove it works — they might be lucky — but because the egregore can't survive a counterexample.

Three — I take the smallest step the dome says shouldn't be allowed. Not a leap. A nudge. And then I notice the world didn't end. The dome is mostly maintained by the fact that nobody tests it.

`[B-ROLL: Finger pressing against frosted glass, a small crack forming]`
