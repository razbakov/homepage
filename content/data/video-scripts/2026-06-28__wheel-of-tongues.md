# Daily — The Wheel of Tongues

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-06-18-wheel-of-tongues

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Does your language build your mind? That question has haunted me since I started thinking in my second tongue. Most of what you'll read about it online is a horoscope. But underneath the horoscope — there's a wheel. And it might be a mirror.

## Point 1 — The horoscope problem (0:20–1:10)

Here's the uncomfortable part first. Almost every "how cultures think" framework hands each people only virtues. Depth, harmony, resilience, wisdom — nobody gets a shadow, nobody gets a cost. It's flattering enough that everyone nods. Psychologists call it the Barnum effect. Same trick as horoscopes.

And the big seductive claim — that your grammar builds your worldview — in its strong form, it's mostly dead. Bilinguals think fine in both heads. Translation works. What survives is a weaker version: grammar sets your defaults of attention. Not a wall. A worn groove.

Out of all the cultural psychology research, only four dimensions actually replicate. Individualism versus collectivism. High versus low context. Analytic versus holistic attention. Tight versus loose norms. Everything else — soul, beauty, poetry — that's a culture's self-image. Wonderful for empathy. Useless for prediction. Never read a person off the map.

## Point 2 — The myth that steers (1:10–2:10)

But here's where it gets interesting. A myth is not a failed fact. You don't take a poem to a lab to ask whether it's accurate — you ask what it keeps alive.

There is no Lion in the night sky. And sailors steered by it for three thousand years. The error is never believing in the Lion. The error is going hunting for it.

So in the essay I built a wheel — nineteen cultures, nineteen first questions. Germany asks, "Is it correct?" Cuba asks, "Who is coming?" Iran asks, "What deeper truth?" Thailand asks, "Can we keep it light?" Not a taxonomy. A mandala. The faculties of one human psyche, handed out to different peoples to keep awake on behalf of the whole species. Each culture becomes a priest of one faculty. And the language — the grammar itself — is the instrument that keeps that faculty in tune.

## Point 3 — The wheel in you (2:10–3:05)

Now the personal part. That wheel out there — it's also a diagnostic for the wheel in here. Read it slowly and you'll feel which faculties you keep lit without trying. And which ones went quiet years ago.

The systems-thinker who never sleeps. The host who always asks who's coming. The releaser — the one who can set a heavy thing down — maybe gone silent for a long time.

And if you're someone who lives across several of these circuits — who thinks in one language and dreams in another, who counts in one and loves in another — hear this: you are not divided. A mind that runs several grammars is built for the two highest faculties on the list. To question. And to weave.
