# Daily — There Are No Fairy Tales — Everything Imagined Has Already Happened Somewhere

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-no-fairy-tales-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Name something fictional. A dragon. A city under the sea. A god with an animal head. Now — why does every culture on Earth imagine the same things? I don't think we make stories up. I think we receive them.

## Point 1 — The same myths everywhere (0:20–1:10)

Here's what got me. Flood stories — Mesopotamia and the Andes. Sky-fathers and earth-mothers on continents that never traded. Dragons curled around mountains in Norway and dragons curled around mountains in China. Sunken cities — Atlantis, Lemuria, Mu. Always a sunken city. Always.

If imagination were really invention from nothing, you'd expect the inventions to look different. But they don't. You see the same furniture rearranged. The same archetypes wearing local clothes.

Jung called it the collective unconscious. He was close. But he kept it inside the skull — some shared psychic layer baked into the species. I think it's bigger than that. I don't think it's inside the skull. I think the skull is tuned to it.

`[B-ROLL: World map with lines connecting similar myths across continents]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Same signal. Different costumes."]`

## Point 2 — The brain as antenna (1:10–2:10)

Here's the flip. What if the brain isn't a factory? What if it's a receiver?

It doesn't generate from nothing — we have no evidence that it does. What it does, beautifully, constantly, is receive, filter, interpret. Every creative act is closer to translation than invention.

So when a writer imagines a dragon — a cave painter draws a long-bodied beast with claws, a Tang-dynasty scribe writes a serpent of the sky, Tolkien writes Smaug. Same signal. Different costumes.

And once you flip that — once imagination is reception, not invention — everything has to be reread. Atlantis isn't a lie. It's a distorted description of something real, maybe in another timeline. UFOs aren't paranoia. They're sightings rendered in the visual vocabulary of the era — airships in the 1890s, flying saucers in the 1950s, glowing tic-tacs in the 2010s.

This is why writers have always made power nervous. Not because they invent — because they receive. They pull material from a layer the official version of reality wants to keep flat.

`[B-ROLL: Close-up of someone writing in a notebook, pen moving fast]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Writers are just better antennae."]`

## Point 3 — What you tune to, you amplify (2:10–3:05)

And this is where it gets personal. If stories aren't made up — if they're received — then what you write matters differently than you thought.

You're not entertaining. You're transmitting. Every story that lands trains other antennae to pick up the same signal. Write enough zombie apocalypses and you train a generation to feel the apocalypse louder than the rebuilding. Write enough dystopias and you make it harder for anyone to receive a good civilization.

The substrate contains everything. Beautiful timelines, ugly ones, weird ones, gentle ones. Whatever you tune to, you amplify. Whatever you amplify, others receive.

So the question stops being "what should I invent?" It becomes "what am I willing to be a witness to?"
