# Daily — The Four Seasons of Wealth

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-07-23-four-seasons-of-wealth

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Money is not a number. Three books keep circling the same table in my head — *Richest Man in Babylon*, *Rich Dad Poor Dad*, *The Cashflow Quadrant* — and when you read them together, they're not about money at all. They're about seasons. And what you're supposed to do with the harvest before winter comes.

## Point 1 — Plant without proof (0:20–1:10)

Here's where it starts. Spring. Clason's one rule underneath all his parables — a part of all you earn is yours to keep. A tenth. Before rent, before food, before anything touches it. That tenth is a seed. And a seed never feels like much — that's the whole point. Spring is the faith season. You act before there's any evidence. You put something in the ground and you trust the ground will answer.

Most people do it backwards. They spend first and try to save what's left. But what's left is always nothing, because life expands to swallow whatever you leave in front of it.

So the first season is just this: plant. Before you have proof. Before it makes sense. That's the only way anything grows.

`[B-ROLL: Hands pressing a seed into dark soil, close-up]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: Pay yourself first. A seed never feels like enough.]`

## Point 2 — Own what feeds you (1:10–2:10)

Now, summer. This is where Kiyosaki's distinction lives. An asset puts money in your pocket. A liability takes it out. Simple. But almost nobody follows it, because liabilities are beautiful. They look like success. The car, the bigger apartment, the thing you carry that only ever asks to be fed.

Summer is the season of owning — and owning means responsibility. You tend what you have. You weed it, guard it, keep it alive through the heat. You are not the richest person because you have the most. You're the richest because what you have is *working*.

And then comes autumn — the harvest. The Cashflow Quadrant. On the left side, you trade time for money. Employee, self-employed. You stop, the income stops. On the right side — business owner, investor — the system produces whether or not your hands are on it. That's the whole migration. Not "work harder." Build something that keeps producing after you step away.

`[B-ROLL: Time-lapse of a garden growing thick through summer, then hands gathering fruit]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: The harvest is not a wage. A wage ends when the arm stops swinging.]`

## Point 3 — The season none of them wrote (2:10–3:05)

Here's the part that hit me hardest. None of these books talk about winter. They're all accumulation — summer and autumn skills. But wealth without winter — without rest, without passing it on — is just a bigger pile that dies with the person who stacked it.

I've started thinking of business ideas like children. You carry them, you're responsible for them, and their whole purpose is to be handed forward. One good idea, owned across a full season, becomes a legacy. And legacy, by definition, is something you build to give away.

The son can't just be handed the field. He has to leave first — discover something on his own — and only then can he come home and receive what only the father holds. That double movement, leave to discover, return to inherit, is how a harvest survives the man who grew it.
