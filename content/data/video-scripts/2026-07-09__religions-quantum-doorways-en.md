# Daily — Every Religion Describes the Same Quantum World From a Different Doorway

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-religions-quantum-doorways-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Religions aren't arguing. That's the thought I keep coming back to. They're standing around the same room, each looking through a different doorway — and disagreeing only because nobody can see the whole room from where they're standing. The disagreement is geometry, not error.

## Point 1 — Five doorways, one room (0:20–1:10)

Christianity walks in through a moral door. The room looks like a courtroom — sin, grace, judgment, redemption. There are beings everywhere: angels, demons, saints. It's a hierarchy at war.

Buddhism walks in through an experiential door. Same room, but now the question isn't "who's righteous?" — it's "what is this experience made of?" Suffering, attachment, emptiness.

Islam walks in through order. Angels with specific jobs. Jinn — not metaphors, a whole category of beings with their own choices. And here's what's wild: the jinn a Muslim describes? A shaman recognizes them immediately. Different name, same thing.

Hinduism walks in through the widest door and refuses to close it. A thousand gods — but every single one described as an aspect of the one. And shamanism? The oldest door. Before scripture, before theology. Spirits everywhere. No worship — negotiation.

Five traditions. Five vocabularies. One room.

`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Five traditions. One room."]`

## Point 2 — The pattern underneath (1:10–2:10)

Here's what made me stop and pay attention. Take any of these systems — any one — and the structure is identical. There are beings that aren't human. They're organized into hierarchies or ecologies. Some help, some harm, most are neutral. Humans interact with them whether they know it or not. And the interaction is governed by something — moral law, karma, balance, contract.

The names are different because each tradition crystallized in a different culture, a different language, a different stack of inherited symbols. But the kind of thing being described? Same thing. Christianity's archangel and Hinduism's deva and shamanism's helper-spirit — not literally identical, but the same category of being seen from three different sides. Like calling a translucent stone "blue" from the north and "green" from the south.

You know the elephant parable. Yes, it's overused. But the point isn't that the blind men are wrong. Each one is correct about exactly what they're touching. The trunk really is like a snake. The error is concluding that the elephant is only a snake.

`[B-ROLL: Overhead diagram — five labeled doorways around a single room, lines converging to center]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Same category. Different sides."]`

## Point 3 — Not relativism (2:10–3:05)

Now — this is not me saying every map is equally valid, pick whatever feels nice. That's relativism, and that's lazy. The maps are not equal. A Christian looking at jinn through Christian categories collapses them all into "demons" and loses information. A shaman looking at the Trinity sees three powerful spirits and misses the structural claim entirely.

What's equal is the territory. What's unequal is each map's resolution at any given coordinate. So I stopped asking "which religion is true?" — that question is malformed. Instead: what is each tradition unusually good at describing? Christianity — moral structure. Buddhism — the phenomenology of mind. Islam — the architecture of the unseen. Read them all. Where they overlap, that's probably load-bearing. Where they diverge, one map probably has better resolution than another at that exact spot.
