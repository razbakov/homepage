# Daily — Why I Stopped Treating Myself Like a One-Job Person

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-not-one-job-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

I spent years using willpower to not care about half of my own life. I had the title, the salary, the clean LinkedIn — and I was slowly becoming miserable. Today I want to talk about why I stopped treating myself like a one-job person. Because the one-job rule? Nobody's enforcing it but you.

## Point 1 — The Leaks (0:20–1:10)

Here's how it worked for me. I picked coding. Went deep. Senior engineer, engineering manager, CTO. The path delivered exactly what it promised — money, legibility, a clean answer at dinner parties. But every time I let myself care about something outside the lane — dance, philosophy, community building, writing — there was this voice: *that's a distraction, get back to the main thing.*

I treated those interests like leaks in the system. The system being my career. The leaks being… me. And at some point I noticed — most of my energy wasn't going into the work. It was going into plugging the leaks. I was spending willpower to suppress the parts of myself that actually made me alive. That's an expensive way to live.

## Point 2 — Leonardo's Permission (1:10–2:10)

The shift didn't come from a productivity book. It came from Leonardo da Vinci. Not the romantic genius painter version — the working pattern. This man was a painter, an anatomist, an engineer, a stage designer, a botanist, a notebook addict. And none of it was a hobby. It was all the work. You open his notebooks and there's a heart valve sketch next to a flying machine next to a recipe next to a flood diagram. He didn't apologize for the range. He didn't try to focus. The range was his discipline.

And the thing that got me wasn't his talent. It was the permission. He just let himself be that. So I started asking — what if I stopped fighting my own range? What if doing many things isn't the problem to solve? What if it's the operating mode?

## Point 3 — The Lattice (2:10–3:05)

So today my week has WeDance, AI agents, dance festivals, essays, coding, philosophical writing about governance. A career coach would say I'm spreading thin. Here's what I've actually noticed by living it: each domain feeds the others. The AI agents exist because I needed someone to run the dance community. The community exists because my philosophy needed a real test case. The philosophy exists because building products without a worldview produces products I don't respect. It's not five careers. It's one career shaped like a lattice.

I'll be honest — there's a cost. No clean narrative. Recruiters can't classify me. Every project moves slower than it would solo. But the cost of the alternative — pretending half of me doesn't exist so my LinkedIn parses cleanly — that cost was not bearable. And most people I talk to? They have the same shape underneath. One job they're paid for, four things they actually care about, and they're waiting until they're "successful enough" to stop choking those parts of themselves.
