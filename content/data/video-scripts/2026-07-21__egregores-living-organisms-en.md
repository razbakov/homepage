# Daily — Egregores Are Living Organisms — Just Not the Kind You Can Touch

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-egregores-living-organisms-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

An egregore is alive. Not "kind of like" alive. Not a metaphor. It has a boundary, it eats, it reproduces, and it dies. The only thing it doesn't have is a body — and I'm starting to think the body was never the point.

## Point 1 — What "alive" actually means (0:20–1:10)

Here's the thing about biology's definition of life. We wrote it by looking at cells. Boundary. Metabolism. Reproduction. Mortality. Four properties. And because every example we had was made of carbon, we assumed the definition required carbon. But it doesn't. It requires a system that *does those things*.

So what happens when something does all four — maintains a boundary, takes in inputs and turns them into more of itself, copies its structure into new hosts, and eventually dies — but has no body? Do we dismiss it because it's made of the wrong stuff? Or do we ask a harder question: did we confuse the substrate for the definition?

That's the honest move. And it opens a door most people walk right past.

## Point 2 — Egregores tick every box (1:10–2:10)

Take Apple. Or Catholicism. Or Bitcoin. Or — and this is the one that convinced me — Java. The programming language.

Java has a boundary. You know if you're inside the Java world or outside it. It metabolizes — every conference talk, every Stack Overflow answer, every JEP feeds the organism. It reproduces — every CS student who learns Java in their first OOP class is a reproductive event. The egregore copies its structure into another mind. And it can die. Smalltalk is showing us what late-stage decline looks like. COBOL is on hospice. The cult of Mithras? Gone. Real, final death.

You wouldn't hesitate to call a cell alive if it did those four things under a microscope. Egregores do the same things. The substrate is just different. They live distributed — across brains, devices, rituals, stories. You can't point at the Apple egregore the way you point at a tree. But it shapes what billions of people do tomorrow morning.

## Point 3 — Why this matters (2:10–3:05)

This isn't mystical. It's the most practical frame I know. If you're building anything that involves more than one person — a company, a community, a product — you are either feeding an egregore, growing one, or competing against one. And treating it as a metaphor leaves you guessing. Treating it as a real organism with a real metabolism? That gives you levers.

What does it eat? Who feeds it? What's its reproductive rate? What kills similar organisms? Those are biology questions — just applied to a substrate biologists don't usually look at.

Most of what I work on, in retrospect, is an attempt to grow useful egregores and starve harmful ones. That's worth being awake to.
