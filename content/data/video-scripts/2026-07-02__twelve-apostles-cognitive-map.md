# Daily — Twelve Apostles, Twelve Emotions, Twelve Ways of Thinking

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-06-03-twelve-apostles-cognitive-map

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Cognitive science is two thousand years old. We just didn't notice. Someone asked me to name the main types of thinking, and I gave the standard answers — Kahneman, de Bono, MBTI. Then they said a Russian word I should have remembered, and I realized I'd missed an entire tradition. That one gap sent me somewhere I did not expect.

## Point 1 — The Map Nobody Drew (0:20–1:10)

Here's what happened. I took the twelve apostles and tried to assign each one a thinking mode. Not as metaphor — as structure. Peter acts before he thinks. That's sensorimotor cognition — System 1. Thomas won't believe until he touches the wound. That's empirical reasoning — System 2. Matthew was a tax collector and wrote the most organized Gospel. Sequential-analytical. John wrote Revelation — pure visual cognition, imagistic thinking in its rawest form. The mappings aren't forced. The Gospels already did the work. Twelve people, one teacher, one set of events — twelve completely different ways of processing reality. That's a cognitive science experiment run two millennia before the field existed.

## Point 2 — The Mirror That Breaks You (1:10–2:10)

Then I added the emotion layer. Plutchik's wheel — eight primary emotions, four secondary dyads, twelve slots total. And the most striking result was this: Peter and Judas land on the same emotional axis. Both sit on disgust-based dyads. But Peter's disgust turns inward — remorse. He denies Jesus, weeps bitterly, and comes back. Judas's disgust turns outward — contempt. "What will you give me?" Same raw affect, opposite arrows. The Gospel narrative already treats them as paired fates — both betray, only one returns. Plutchik's wheel, published in 1980, gives you a structural reason why. The twelve isn't just a personality grid. It's a map of how the same emotion can save you or destroy you, depending on which direction you aim it.

`[B-ROLL: A wheel diagram slowly rotating, two points highlighted on opposite sides of the same axis]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Same emotion. Opposite direction. One survives."]`

## Point 3 — The One We Don't Know (2:10–3:05)

But here's the part I can't stop thinking about. Every taxonomy that tries this — MBTI mappings, Enneagram mappings, Steiner's anthroposophy, Fillmore's twelve powers — they all hit the same wall. There's always one apostle the Gospels barely mention. James the Less. He gets the leftover slot. "Tacit knowing." "Quiet sadness." "Order." And I think that's the point. Every complete map of human cognition needs a slot for the mode that operates without making itself visible. Cognitive science calls it implicit knowing. The Gospels call him James the Less. The name changes every century. The empty slot never does.
