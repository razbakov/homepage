# Daily — The Deeper You Can Imagine a Structure, the More You Can Control It

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-imagine-control-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Control is not willpower. It's not discipline. It's not even experience. Control is how deeply you can imagine the thing you're trying to run. If your mental picture is flat, you're not steering — you're guessing. And guessing dressed up as competence is still guessing.

`[B-ROLL: hands adjusting a complex mechanical watch, gears visible]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Control = mental resolution"]`

## Point 1 — The Thin Model (0:20–1:10)

Here's what made this click for me. Think about two surgeons. Same hands, same scalpel, same procedure on paper. One holds a real three-dimensional body in their head — where the bleeders hide, how the fascia slides. The other learned from flat textbook diagrams. Radically different outcomes. Not because one is smarter. Because one is operating on the actual system, and the other is operating on a cartoon of it.

Now run that against everything. A developer who can hold the full architecture — the request lifecycle, the failure modes, what breaks when this service goes offline — writes different code than one who only sees the function in front of them. Same keyboard. Lower resolution. They'll keep introducing bugs the first one literally cannot introduce, because the first one can *see* the collision before they type it.

Delegation works the same way. The shallower your model of the team, the more you micromanage. Micromanagement is what happens when your imagination is too thin to trust.

`[B-ROLL: split screen — detailed architectural blueprint vs. rough napkin sketch]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "You can only control what you can imagine"]`

## Point 2 — The Wizard Effect (1:10–2:10)

This explains something that always bugged me. Why does a twenty-year veteran in any field look like a wizard? They're not smarter. Their internal model just has more layers. More edge cases. More "this looks fine but it's about to break" patterns baked in. They're not predicting the future — they're running a higher-fidelity simulation than everyone else in the room.

And flip it. Why does most strategy fail? Because the strategist had a flat picture of a fractal world. They imagined layer one, maybe layer two. The system has eight. Reality reached into layer five and tore the plan apart.

You cannot beat someone whose mental resolution exceeds yours. You can only get lucky.

`[B-ROLL: chess grandmaster studying a board, calm while opponent fidgets]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "They're not predicting — they're simulating"]`

## Point 3 — The Honest Question (2:10–3:05)

So here's where it gets uncomfortable. If control is bounded by resolution, then the most honest question you can ask yourself is: where is my picture thinnest right now? Which system am I trying to run with a cartoon-level model? My money? My health? A relationship I keep poking at without ever really understanding?

And what would it actually cost to go one layer deeper? A few hours. A hard conversation. A book you've been avoiding. That cost is almost always smaller than you think. And the return — decisions that stop being agonizing, surprises that stop being surprises — is almost always larger.

Imagine deeper. Then look at what just became controllable.
