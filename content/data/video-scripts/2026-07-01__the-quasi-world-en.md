# Daily — The Quasi-World

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-06-13-the-quasi-world-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

What if meaning doesn't live in matter? Not metaphorically — structurally. There's a second world running alongside this one, interwoven with it, pressing against it at every point. And nearly every serious tradition in human history has drawn the same map.

## Point 1 — The Imaginal Is Real (0:20–1:10)

There's a scholar named Henry Corbin who spent his life recovering something the Persian Sufis knew — they called it the *mundus imaginalis*. The imaginal world. And his whole fight was against one word: "imaginary." Because imaginary means made up. Imaginal means *real but not physical*. A world more subtle than matter, less abstract than pure thought. You don't reach it with your hands. You don't reach it with logic. You reach it with the imagination — and Corbin said that's not a toy. It's an organ of knowledge.

Ibn Arabi called the threshold between the two worlds the *barzakh* — the isthmus. The place where two seas meet without mixing. Where the immaterial puts on shape. Where the material becomes spirit. This isn't fringe thinking. This sits inside one of the most carefully worked-out cosmologies ever built. The question isn't whether anyone took this seriously. The question is why we stopped.

## Point 2 — Where Meaning Actually Lives (1:10–2:10)

Here's the part that surprised me. This isn't only mysticism. Hard-nosed analytic philosophers backed into the same room.

Plato put the Forms outside matter because matter can only hold copies, never the meaning itself. Fine — that's Plato. But then Gottlob Frege, the father of modern logic, argued that thoughts — the contents of true propositions — must inhabit a "third realm." Not physical, not private-mental. Objective, shared, but with no mass and no location. Karl Popper called it World 3: the world of meanings, problems, and theories. As real as the physical world. Able to act back on it.

So the mystics say meaning lives in another world. The logicians say the same thing, because they can't make meaning sit anywhere in the physical world either. The quasi-world is Frege's third realm with the lights on — not a dry archive of propositions but a living, populated place. Populated with what? With organisms made of meaning the way your body is made of matter. What you experience as a strong idea, a collective mood, a spirit of the age — that might be what one of these beings looks like from our side.

## Point 3 — The Void Is Still Underneath (2:10–3:05)

The boldest move in the essay is reading Genesis 1 not as history but as cosmology. The void — *tohu va-vohu* — isn't emptiness. It's un-form. The pregnant fullness before differentiation. The Kabbalists said the Infinite contracted itself to make room for a world, and then the first vessels shattered, scattering sparks of meaning down into matter. Sparks still waiting to be gathered.

And here's what lands for me: if the void is always underneath — not an event that happened once — then creation is still happening. Right now. Through you. The stars are the visible seam where the two worlds show through. But maybe you are too. Maybe human beings are one of the places the passage runs. One of the doors where meaning crosses into matter — or fails to. That's the question the essay leaves open. On purpose.
