# Daily — Money Is Just Crystallized Influence — and Influence Is Just Clarity of Vision

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-money-is-influence-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)
Money is not paper. Money is a frozen "yes." Every euro you hold is a yes that someone, somewhere, said — they handed you their attention, their hour, their agreement, and the bank gave you a token to remember it by. The token is portable. The yes is what's actually inside.

## Point 1 — The Chain (0:20–1:10)
I run a bunch of projects — a dance platform, a TV channel, a science evening, a coaching site. Some make money. Some don't. And the ones that make money are never the cleverest or the prettiest. They're the ones where I can say what I'm doing in one breath and a stranger can tell me back what I just said. That's the whole game.

Here's the chain. Vision-clarity gives you simple words. Simple words build trust. Trust means people delegate decisions to you — that's influence. And money? Money is just the part of influence that fits in a wallet.

When someone buys a festival ticket from me, they're saying: go book the venue, hire the DJ, do the thing — I trust the room you'll build. That's delegated authority with a price tag. That's all money is.

## Point 2 — The Wrong End (1:10–2:10)
Now here's the part that took me a while to swallow. Chasing money directly is chasing the wrong end of the chain. It's like trying to make yourself famous by demanding attention. You can, briefly — and people will pull away.

Same with money. You can extract a quick transaction with a clever checkout flow. Next quarter you'll be doing it again from zero. Because nothing accumulated. Nobody's out there telling their friend about your clever checkout.

But if you build clarity — articulate it simply, and demonstrate over years that you do what you say — the asks get bigger on their own. First someone gives you twenty minutes. Then twenty euros. Then a thousand, and they say figure it out. The size of the yes scales with the sharpness of the picture and the consistency of your follow-through. You don't have to push.

Sharpness is the rate-limiter on everything downstream.

## Point 3 — The Stranger Test (2:10–3:05)
So here's the honest test. It's brutally cheap. Tell a stranger what you do, in one sentence, no jargon. Then stop talking. Watch their face.

Three things happen. One — their eyes light up and they ask a question. The picture landed. You're done. Two — they nod politely and change the subject. The picture didn't land. That's your fault, not theirs. Go simplify. Three — and this is the magic one — they repeat your sentence back in their own words and it's *better* than what you said. Save those words. Use them tomorrow. They just did your copywriting for free.

I do this constantly. Strangers on a train, the person next to me at a bar. Not as a sales move — as a calibration tool. That blank politeness in scenario two is the most useful feedback I get all month.
