# Daily — Who Am I? Will, Suffering, and Ikigai

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-06-19-will-and-ikigai

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Who am I? Not the party answer — the job, the city, the list of things you do. The real one. The one that, if you sat with it honestly, would scare you a little. Here's what I think: that question is not philosophy. It's the most practical question you will ever face.

## Point 1 — The blind force (0:20–1:10)

Schopenhauer looked at the world and saw one thing underneath everything. He called it the will. This enormous, tireless energy pushing everything forward — the seed splitting stone, the animal hunting, you reaching for the thing you want before you've even decided to. All-powerful. And completely blind. No direction. Just pure wanting, forever unsatisfied.

That's where the suffering comes from. Not because something went wrong. Because energy without aim only churns.

And that's exactly where ikigai enters. Your mission — the thing that is yours to do — is not a hobby. It's not a nice addition to your life. It is the direction given to all that blind force. You take everything that was just churning and you point it somewhere. The will finally gets a heading.

And the heading has a specific shape. Your mission is the thing that is very difficult for you and impossible for everyone else. Not what's easy — easy is a gift. Not what's merely hard — hard alone is just labour. The mission lives at the edge of what you're capable of. That edge is the direction the will was looking for.

## Point 2 — Suffering is the weather (1:10–2:10)

Now the honest part. Life is suffering. The Buddha opens with it. The scriptures are soaked in it. And we stand there asking the sky — why? As if suffering were a bug. A mistake. An injustice done to us specifically.

It's not a mistake. It's the norm. It's the baseline condition of being a creature made of want. And the moment you stop treating it as an outrage and start treating it as the weather you live in, something relaxes. You stop spending your strength on the protest.

So what do you do inside it? I think there are three exits. Music — it suspends the suffering, reaches under the thinking and quiets you for a few minutes. Empathy — turning toward the other, reconnecting. And meditation — the space between what you feel and what you choose to do about it. That space is everything. The feeling is not in your control. The reaction is.

`[B-ROLL: Rain on a window, then hands on piano keys]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The question was never why. It's what you do inside it."]`

## Point 3 — Answer it alone first (2:10–3:05)

But here's the part that trips people up. The order matters. You cannot turn outward — toward empathy, toward others — until you've turned inward first. The person who runs to help before they've found themselves isn't giving. They're fleeing the question.

First connect with yourself. Answer it. Who am I. Then turn outward. That sequence is not optional.

And when you do answer it — when the mission clicks — something shifts. The energy that was only suffering becomes the energy that carries you. Not happiness. Not comfort. The will with a direction at last. That's ikigai.
