# Daily — From Vision to Reality: How Resources, Trust, and Delegated Power Compound

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-vision-to-reality-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Nobody followed my vision. Not at first. Because a vision nobody can see isn't a vision — it's a fantasy. The day I stopped announcing what I wanted to build and started spending my own money, my own weekends, my own reputation on it — that's the day things got real. Here's the thing nobody tells you about leadership: you don't earn it by talking. You earn it by going first.

## Point 1 — Go First With Your Own Resources (0:20–1:10)

WeDance didn't start with a co-founder or a pitch deck. It started with me buying a domain, building the first version on weekends, and bankrolling events out of my own pocket. I showed up on a dance floor in Munich week after week before anyone cared.

And that's the pattern. Every single thing I've shipped that mattered followed the same shape. Vision first. Then your own resources. Then visible results. Then — and only then — other people start adding theirs.

Most people stall at step one. They have the vision, and they wait. They wait for a partner, a budget, a better moment. While they wait, the vision stays decorative. It's a thing they describe at dinner parties.

The break happens the day you stop waiting and put something on the line — money, time, reputation, social capital. Not as a sacrifice. As the first proof of conviction. Money is fuel, not the engine. The engine is willingness to bet before the world agrees with you.

## Point 2 — Visible Results Change the Equation (1:10–2:10)

Once the thing exists — a website, an event, a product — it stops being your private idea and becomes something people can actually evaluate. And that changes everything.

Before the artifact, you're someone with an idea. People are polite. Vaguely interested. Unwilling to spend anything on you. After the artifact, you're someone who shipped. Now they can see whether the quality matches the way you talk. They can see whether you finish things or only start them. And the ones who like what they see? They start adding their own resources.

This isn't charity. It's the same logic you used. They're investing in something visible because the visible thing reduces the risk. You de-risked it for them by going first.

WeDance grew this way. Dancegods grew this way. The Munich dance community I'm part of now grew this way. Each one started with me putting in work nobody asked me to do. And at some point, other people decided it was real enough to add their own work to it.

`[B-ROLL: A growing event — small room becomes a packed venue, social media posts gaining traction, people tagging each other]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "You de-risked it by going first."]`

## Point 3 — Trust Compounds Into Delegated Power (2:10–3:05)

Here's where it gets interesting. Two things compound simultaneously. The artifact — every event, every post, every version you release. And trust. Trust is quieter, but it's the one that changes your life.

The first event, I had to convince every single attendee. The tenth event, people just showed up. The history did the work. I didn't have to re-earn anything from scratch.

And past a certain threshold, something strange happens. People stop asking you to convince them. They start handing you decisions. "You pick the venue." "You decide the format." "If you say this is the direction, we're in." That's real leadership. Not because you asserted authority. Because people decided, quietly and individually, that your judgment in this domain is worth trusting. You can't argue your way into that. It arrives on its own — after the artifact, the results, and the trust have been compounding long enough.
