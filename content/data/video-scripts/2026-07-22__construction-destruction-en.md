# Daily — Construction and Destruction: The Two Forces Behind Every Idea That Becomes Real

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-construction-destruction-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Nothing gets built without something else getting torn down. That's the line I keep coming back to — in code, in relationships, in every product I've ever shipped. We celebrate the construction. We edit out the destruction. But they're the same motion.

## Point 1 — The Shadow Ledger (0:20–1:10)

When I shipped the first version of WeDance, I wrote a long thread about what we built. I didn't write a single line about what it killed. It killed my evenings. It killed a friendship that had been coasting on shared free time. It killed the version of me who could still say he was "just exploring." None of that is bad. It's just true.

Every founder I respect has a list like this. They don't talk about it because the construction side is what gets retweeted. The destruction side feels like complaining. So we edit it out. And then every new project feels harder than expected — because we budget for the build but never for what the build consumes.

`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "We budget for the build. Never for what it consumes."]`

## Point 2 — Destruction Is a Craft (1:10–2:10)

You see this most clearly in code. You add a feature. It changes a function called from forty places. Some things adapt, some break silently, and some were workarounds for the absence of the thing you just added. Dead weight. But nobody deletes them — because deletion feels destructive.

That's where most engineers freeze. They've been trained to construct. Not to destroy with the same care. The best engineers I learn from? They delete more than they write. They look at a pull request and ask: what is this making redundant? What old assumption can we bury now? The codebase shrinks under their hands and gets stronger. That's the craft nobody teaches.

And it's not just code. When you ship a feature that solves a real pain, you also kill the scaffolding people built around that pain. The spreadsheet someone maintained for three years. The Slack channel. The mental model your power users carefully constructed. Some will thank you. Some will be furious — not because your feature is bad, but because you erased the thing they were proud of.

`[B-ROLL: Code diff showing more red (deletions) than green (additions)]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The best engineers delete more than they write."]`

## Point 3 — Name What You Kill (2:10–3:05)

This goes way beyond software. Saying yes to one person quietly says no to everyone you didn't choose. Moving in together destroys the version of life where you both still had your own space. Getting clear about what you want destroys the polite ambiguity that was doing all the diplomatic work.

The relationships I've watched fall apart — they didn't fail because of what was built. They failed because nobody would name what had been killed to build it.

So here's what I try to do now. Every meaningful decision — I write down what it constructs and what it destroys. Same page, both columns. Sometimes the destruction column is heavy enough to reframe the whole decision. Not to talk myself out of it. The opposite. To honor that the choice is real. A yes that doesn't notice what it's killing isn't really a yes. It's a vague hope.
