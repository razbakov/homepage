# Daily — After Babylon: From Nations to Ideas

Borders are disappearing — and nobody's mourning them. Here's what I mean: the thing that holds people together now isn't geography. It's an idea, a brand, a shared question. So I went back to the oldest story I know about why we split in the first place.

## Point 1 — The Babylon Pattern (0:20–1:10)

In the Bible there's Babylon — and the way I read it, Babylon isn't just a city. It's a blueprint. Empire takes one land, breaks it into smaller pieces. But splitting the map isn't enough — people remember they were one. So you give them different languages. Different cultures. And over generations, they forget.

Think about Latin. The Roman Empire pushes Latin everywhere — for trade, for law, it's useful, so people adopt it. But at home? At home you keep speaking what you feel. Then the empire fades, and the imposed language drifts. It mixes with what was kept. New languages are born. French, Spanish, Portuguese — all children of a tool of separation that kept working centuries after the empire that built it was gone.

That's the pattern. Divide. Let time do the rest.

## Point 2 — Nations as Thinking Types (1:10–2:10)

Here's where it gets interesting. I've come to believe that different cultures are really about different types of thinking. And each thinking type has a first question — the question the mind reaches for before anything else.

Germany asks: *Is it correct?* Russia asks: *What does it mean?* Kenya asks: *How do we rise as one?* America asks: *What's next?*

I hold these loosely — they're a lens, not a verdict. But it reframes what a nation actually is. It's not just a flag or a border. It's a default question. And for a long time, sharpening that question was the project — develop your nation, take pride in your way of thinking.

But here's the canary in the coal mine for me: national football. You know what national football really is? It's simulated war. Nation against nation — who would win this fight? We decided actual war isn't cool, so we play football instead. And I noticed — it doesn't excite me the way it used to. If the simulation stops mattering, the thing it simulates is losing its grip.

`[B-ROLL: Stadium crowd waving national flags, slowly cross-dissolving into a co-working space with people from different backgrounds collaborating]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "A nation is a default question."]`

## Point 3 — Mixing Cocktails (2:10–3:05)

So if the era of sharpening one nation's single question is ending — what comes next? We mix. We put all the questions in the same room. *Is it correct?* and *What does it mean?* and *How do we rise?* and *What's next?* — all at the same table.

Not everyone likes this. You can see the pushback — the pull toward closed borders, the fear of losing identity. I get it. It's the friction of a transition. But I think the future is the mix. Cross-functional teams across cultures, across thinking types.

That's not the loss of identity. It's what comes after Babylon.
