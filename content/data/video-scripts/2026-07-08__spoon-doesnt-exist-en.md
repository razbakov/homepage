# Daily — The Spoon Doesn't Exist: How Logic Builds the Cage You Can't Escape

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-05-08-spoon-doesnt-exist-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

Every smart person I know is trapped — and the trap is built from their own intelligence. That's the idea I can't stop thinking about. There's a line from The Matrix: "there is no spoon." Most people file it under movie magic. I think it's the most precise piece of philosophy in any blockbuster. Here's why.

## Point 1 — The cage is made of logic (0:20–1:10)

You learn a framework — Newton's physics, React and microservices, Y Combinator startup wisdom — and it works. It works so well that you stop noticing it's a lens. It starts feeling like the world itself. And then anything that doesn't fit inside your framework becomes "impossible." Not impossible everywhere. Impossible inside the model you accepted and forgot you accepted. That's what a cage is. Not iron bars. A logical structure with no exit door inside its own axioms. A PhD is a four-to-seven-year exercise in building one of these. The output is real expertise. The hidden cost is the framework becomes invisible. A dropout might be wrong about a hundred things. A PhD is wrong about one thing, deeply, with a forty-page bibliography defending the wrongness.

`[B-ROLL: Someone working at a desk surrounded by stacked books and monitors — zooming out to reveal the walls closing in]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "A cage is a logical structure with no exit door inside its own axioms."]`

## Point 2 — I've watched myself do this (1:10–2:10)

I've shipped enough code to know this firsthand. You absorb a stack, and within a year every problem arrives pre-shaped to fit that stack. Your brain stops asking "what does this need to be?" and starts asking "which of my existing components can I press into service?" Things that would be trivial in a different paradigm become "hard" — not because they are, but because the path runs through territory your logic already labeled off-map. I've rejected ideas not because they were bad, but because they didn't compile inside the framework I was running. And it's not just code. I have friends with multiple degrees who can articulate, in beautiful detail, exactly why they cannot quit the job they hate. The articulation is the prison. The argument is airtight. That's how you know it's a cage.

`[B-ROLL: Hands typing code, then pulling back to show the same screen reflected endlessly in mirrors]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The argument is airtight. That's how you know it's a cage."]`

## Point 3 — Taking off glasses you didn't know you wore (2:10–3:05)

Every limiting belief I've ever dropped had the same structure. Not "I can't do X." More like — I built such a thorough case for how X works that the version where it works differently isn't even a thought I can finish. Software has to be built by employees. Income has to come from a job. Audiences require consistency on one platform. Every time I stepped out, it didn't feel like learning something new. It felt like taking off a pair of glasses I didn't know I was wearing. You can't think your way out of this — the cage is made of thinking. But you can watch for the signals. When the word "impossible" shows up, ask: impossible everywhere, or impossible inside a framework I accepted at twenty-three? Hang out with people whose worldview doesn't compile in your head. The crash is the gift.
