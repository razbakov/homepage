## Point 1 — The Leonardo shape (0:20–1:10)

Leonardo da Vinci didn't have a job. He had an obsession with everything that moved or held still. He painted, he dissected bodies by candlelight, he designed war machines, he filled pages with water turbulence. And here's the thing — his anatomy lessons changed his paintings. His water studies changed his anatomy. His war machines came from watching birds turn in the wind. The connections weren't decoration on top of his work. The connections *were* his work.

Tesla, Newton, Einstein — same pattern. The people who saw furthest were almost never specialists. And it shows up too often to be coincidence.

So here's what I suspect: novel ideas don't come from going deeper into one structure. They come from carrying multiple structures inside you at once — deeply enough that they start to leak into each other.

`[B-ROLL: Da Vinci notebook pages — anatomy, machines, water — dissolving into each other]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: The connections were the work.]`

## Point 2 — The quantum layer (1:10–2:10)

People talk about being "T-shaped" — broad on top, one deep vertical. I think that's the compromise you make when you're too scared to say what you actually mean. What I see in the people I admire is more like an asterisk. Multiple deep verticals sharing one person.

Think of it this way. If novel ideas live at a layer where domains meet, then immersing in only one domain is like building a single-channel radio in a world where the signal broadcasts across many frequencies at once. You'll hear something. You won't hear everything.

Specialists polish one tunnel until they can see grains of dust in the rock. Polymaths see less deep — but they make connections nobody else can. That's not efficient. It's not what hiring managers want. But it might be the actual shape of how breakthroughs happen.

`[B-ROLL: Radio dial scanning across frequencies — static, then suddenly clear signal]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: One channel in a multi-frequency world.]`

## Point 3 — The personal cost (2:10–3:05)

I notice this in my own life. I dance six nights a week some weeks — salsa, timba. I write code. I run AI agents. I host community events. I write philosophy essays. None of that looks related on a resume. All of it feeds each other constantly.

When I built my AI agent team, the architecture came from how a Cuban dance circle works — one leader, many roles, everyone knows when to step in. When I think about code, I think about how a body learns a new movement — slow, conscious, then suddenly automatic.

The cost is real. People ask "what do you do?" and I watch their face flicker. I've been told I'm scattered. Maybe. Or maybe the reason I see these connections is the same reason I'm harder to fit on a business card. Leonardo wasn't a painter who happened to do other things. He was a person whose painting came from doing other things. That's a different kind of life.
