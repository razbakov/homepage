# Daily — The Counterfeit Courier

**Source:** https://razbakov.com/blog/2026-06-14-the-counterfeit-courier-en

## Hook (0:00–0:20)

The most dangerous person in the room is the one everyone respects. Not the villain — the one with the casserole dish. A psychiatrist named M. Scott Peck wrote about this in 1983, and I've been carrying his book like a stone in my pocket ever since. He called it evil. He meant something very specific.

## Point 1 — The Impeccable Surface (0:20–1:10)

Peck wasn't writing about monsters. He was writing about a couple who gave their depressed son — for Christmas — the rifle his older brother had killed himself with. The signature on the card was correct. The wrapping paper was tasteful. Inside was the gun. That's the image I can't shake. Because his whole book is built on this gap — between what the surface says and what actually arrives.

I realized I've been writing about this same gap for months. Four essays, four angles, one machine. In one I called it the quasi-world — how meaning gets corrupted in transit. In another, fragmentation — how fear, running unsupervised, optimizes for the lie. Peck gave me the missing word. The person doing this is a counterfeit courier. They deliver something that looks like meaning. It's wrapped correctly. And inside — it's hollow.

`[B-ROLL: hands carefully wrapping a gift — cut to the box being opened, empty inside]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The harm is the gap between the signature and the contents."]`

## Point 2 — The Autopilot Problem (1:10–2:10)

Here's where it gets uncomfortable for people like me. I've spent months building systems — agents, automations, dashboards — to handle the noise so I can focus on the signal. And I still believe in that. But there's a Peck-shaped failure mode hiding in it.

The inbox is at zero. The calendar is full. The dashboards are green. Everything looks healthy. And the things that are actually difficult — the conversation that scares you, the person breaking quietly because you haven't been in the room — those aren't handled by the autopilot. They can't be. They were never going to be. Because the design spec was frictionless operation. And those things are the friction.

So you end up with a perfectly polished surface. And Peck would ask the same question of a beautifully automated life that he asked of a beautifully respectable one: what does this surface make it possible for you not to look at?

`[B-ROLL: green dashboard metrics, all indicators up — then a slow pull back revealing an empty room]`
`[TEXT ON SCREEN: "A dashboard is not an attended life."]`

## Point 3 — The Unbearable Small Thing (2:10–3:05)

The way out, in Peck's book, is not heroic. It's small and unbearable. Touch the wall to see if it's wall or curtain. Let in another person's pain without inserting a screen. Say out loud, to one person, the sentence the system inside you has spent years optimizing against.

A photograph of a good father is not a good father. A ring is not a marriage. A theological vocabulary is not the act of looking someone in the face. Meaning has to be delivered intact. That's the whole thing.

And the hardest part — the part the book sneaks up on — is that the counterfeit courier is often in the mirror. Not as a villain. As a small structural habit. The habit of letting the wrapping be the gift.
