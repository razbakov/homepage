One monitor is not enough. Two monitors are not enough. What I actually need is *space* — physical, three-dimensional space to think in.

I've been circling this idea for a while now, and I think the best interface for AI is virtual reality.

## The Problem With Flat Screens

We think spatially. We remember where things are. But our current tools trap us in flat rectangles, forcing every thought through a keyboard bottleneck. Typing is slow. Clicking is faster. Speaking is faster still.

But dancing? Dancing is the fastest of all.

## Muscle Memory as Navigation

Picture this: you're standing in a virtual workspace. Every project lives somewhere in the space around you. You step forward — that's your current task. A quick turn to the right — you're in a different project entirely. Your body *remembers* the moves. No file trees. No tab switching. Just muscle memory guiding you to the right place.

It's like choreography for knowledge work. Each move means something. You rehearse the routine until navigation becomes unconscious, and all your attention goes to the work itself.

## Generative Interfaces: Speak and It Appears

Now layer in AI. You speak, and an interface materializes in front of you — right there, at that spot in space. You walk to a new position, speak again, and a different tool appears. The interfaces stay where you summoned them, like objects left on a workbench.

This is a generative environment. Nothing is pre-built. Everything is conjured on demand through conversation.

Think Minecraft, but for productivity. You have one object here, another object there. You pick them up, compare them, merge them, fork them. The environment reshapes itself around your intentions.

## Spells and Artifacts

Here's where the metaphor clicks into place: **prompts are spells, and they create artifacts.**

You speak an incantation — a prompt — and something materializes. A dashboard. A draft. A data visualization. These are artifacts, conjured through language and shaped through movement. The workspace is your spell book, and every corner holds something you've summoned before.

We're not building software interfaces anymore. We're building a magic system.

## Why Dance Is the Answer

Typing is a kind of dancing — fingers moving in learned patterns across keys. Vim users know this. Stenographers know this. But it's dancing constrained to a tiny surface.

Virtual reality removes that constraint. Your whole body becomes the input device. Voice for creation. Hands for manipulation. Movement for navigation. The bandwidth of human expression finally matches the bandwidth of what AI can generate.

We speak faster than we type. We move faster than we speak. And we dance faster than we move with purpose — because dance is movement *made fluid*.

## What This Could Look Like

You put on a headset. You're standing in an empty space. You say: "Show me this week's priorities." Cards appear in front of you at arm's reach. You grab one, pull it closer, say: "Break this down." Sub-tasks fan out like a hand of cards. You step left into your design space, where yesterday's mockups are still floating where you left them. You gesture to merge two of them. You step back to see the full picture.

Every session leaves traces in space. Every return trip starts with familiar landmarks. The environment accumulates your thinking like a studio accumulates sketches.

## The Magic World

We're building something that doesn't have a name yet. It's not an app. It's not an OS. It's a *place* — a place where language creates reality, movement is meaning, and the interface is your body.

Prompts are spells. Outputs are artifacts. And the people using them? They're not users.

They're wizards who dance.
